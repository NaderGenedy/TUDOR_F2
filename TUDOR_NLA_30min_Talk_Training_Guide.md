## F — FAMILIAL: Why FH Kills When Missed (0:00-3:00)

**[Stage direction: Stand still. Title slide or no slide. Eye contact with the room. This is your opening — the room decides in 30 seconds whether they like you.]**

> Thank you. Before I show you a single number, I want to tell you about a man who would have been alive today if we had found him.

> Forty-six years old. Non-smoker. Normal BMI. Played football every weekend. Dropped dead of an anterior STEMI in the changing room. Post-mortem showed severe three-vessel disease that had been building silently for twenty years. His total cholesterol had been flagged as "a bit high" at a GP check four years earlier. Nobody followed it up. Nobody asked why. Nobody scored him.

> He had familial hypercholesterolaemia. He was never diagnosed.

**[Pause — two full seconds of silence. Let the room feel it.]**

> He is not unusual. He is the norm. FH affects approximately 1 in 250 people — more common than type 1 diabetes. Globally, fewer than 10 percent of carriers have been identified. In many countries, fewer than 1 percent.

> Beheshti's meta-analysis in Circulation 2020 shows something that should haunt every preventive cardiologist in this room. Age-stratified FH prevalence climbs through midlife, peaks at about 0.35 percent, and then falls.

**[Beat]**

> FH is genetic. It does not remit. It does not get better with age. That falling tail is not biology resolving. It is carriers dying. The inverted U IS the mortality signal.

**[Shift tone — from gravity to hope]**

> But here is what makes this a solvable tragedy rather than an inevitable one. Luirink, New England Journal of Medicine 2019: statins started in childhood restore near-normal cardiovascular outcomes at twenty-year follow-up. The disease is lethal if missed. It is manageable if found. The cure exists. It is generic. It costs pennies.

> So the question that brought me here today is not "is FH important?" The question is: in 2026, why are our diagnostic tools still failing — and can we build one that works?

**[Time check: you should be at approximately 2:45-3:00]**

## I — INSTRUMENTS FAILED: Our Tools Are Obsolete (3:00-6:30)

**[Advance to your timeline slide — the one with diagnostic criteria on top and drugs on bottom. This is your strongest visual.]**

> This slide tells the whole story, and I think it is the most important image in the talk.

**[Point to top row]**

> Top timeline — when our diagnostic criteria were created. Simon Broome, 1991. MEDPED, 1993. Dutch Lipid Clinic Network, World Health Organization, 1999. All excellent instruments. All calibrated on a specific kind of patient: untreated, with dramatic LDL-C values, tendon xanthomata, corneal arcus, and detailed family pedigrees available at the bedside.

**[Point to bottom row]**

> Bottom timeline — the drugs. Lovastatin. Pravastatin. Atorvastatin. Then the revolution: rosuvastatin, ezetimibe, PCSK9 inhibitors, bempedoic acid, inclisiran. We got spectacularly good at lowering LDL-C.

> And here is the irony that should make every lipidologist in this room slightly uncomfortable. The criteria lean on physical signs and extreme untreated lipid levels. The drugs erased both. We cured the clue before we made the diagnosis. We invented better and better erasers for the exact phenotype our tools were trained to recognise.

**[Slow down for the punchline — this is quotable]**

> The criteria are excellent. The patients they were calibrated for are no longer the patients we see.

> And who IS the modern patient? In our Wales data: type 2 diabetes now at 27 percent among FH probands. BMI around 29. Triglycerides converging above 2 mmol/L. And the number that kills every classical criterion: 62 to 81 percent are already on a statin before the diagnostic question is even asked. The LDL signal has been pharmacologically suppressed before we walk into the room.

**[Advance to the AUC bar chart showing DLCN, MEDPED, Simon Broome across subgroups]**

> So we ran the experiment. DLCN, MEDPED, Simon Broome — head-to-head — in five clinical subgroups the contemporary lipid clinic actually sees.

**[Sweep across the bars, read the numbers]**

> Young patients: all three near 0.50 — chance. On statin at first visit: DLCN barely above the line, MEDPED collapses. Mixed dyslipidaemia — high LDL and high triglycerides: all three fail. And type 2 diabetes — this is 45,715 UK Biobank participants — DLCN scores 0.435.

**[Let it land]**

> That is not "underwhelming." That is worse than flipping a coin. You would improve your diagnostic accuracy by firing the tool and tossing a ten-pence piece.

> I say this with genuine respect for the people who built these criteria. They were right — for 1995. The patient walked off and the tool stayed put.

## D — DISCOVERY: The Triglyceride Asymmetry (9:00-13:00)

**[This is the scientific centrepiece. Take your time. Show the NMR stratification slide first.]**

> Here is the discovery that underpins the entire model, and I want to walk through it carefully because it is not a statistical trick — it is molecular biology.

> We took every untreated UK Biobank participant and sorted them into clinical LDL bands — from under 1.4 all the way to above 4.9 mmol/L. Within each band, we compared FH-positive carriers — the red bars — against FH-negative matched participants — the navy bars. Same LDL. The question: is there anything that distinguishes them when LDL is held constant?

**[Trace the hero panel with your hand]**

> Look at triglycerides. At every single LDL stratum — from the lowest to the highest — FH carriers have lower triglycerides than non-carriers with the same LDL. The delta ranges from minus 0.13 to minus 0.24 mmol/L. It is the same direction, every band, no exceptions.

**[Point down to the control panels]**

> Now the controls. VLDL-cholesterol — unchanged between carriers and non-carriers. HDL-cholesterol — unchanged. The only difference, at matched LDL, is the triglycerides.

**[Slow down — this is the conceptual revelation]**

> Why? Because FH is a selective LDL-receptor lesion. The mutation breaks LDL clearance — that is why LDL goes up. But the triglyceride-rich lipoprotein pathway — VLDL production, lipolysis, remnant clearance — is intact. The liver's triglyceride machinery keeps humming. So you get isolated, pure LDL elevation with normal or low triglycerides.

> Contrast that with polygenic or metabolic hypercholesterolaemia — insulin resistance, hepatic VLDL overproduction. There, LDL rises because the whole triglyceride-rich axis is running hot. LDL goes up AND triglycerides go up. It is a mixed picture.

> The ratio — LDL divided by triglycerides plus 0.1 — we call it the Trig Filter — reads this difference directly off a routine blood test. High Trig Filter: pure LDL elevation, likely receptor defect, likely FH. Low Trig Filter: mixed dyslipidaemia, likely metabolic, likely not monogenic FH.

**[Advance to the propensity-matched slide]**

> Now, a good sceptic is already muttering "confounding." Fair. So we matched for it. Propensity-matched carriers to non-carriers, one to five, on age, sex, and BMI — 13,447 matched UKB participants, balanced on all measured confounders.

> After matching:

**[Confident, rapid-fire through the panels]**

> Panel A — clinical LDL: elevated in carriers. SMD plus 0.27, p less than 10 to the minus 28.

> Panel D — triglycerides: unchanged. p equals 0.07 — non-significant.

> Panel E — HDL-cholesterol: unchanged. p equals 0.99.

> And Panel F — the Trig Filter: elevated 10 percent in carriers. p equals 5.2 times 10 to the minus 25.

**[Beat — land this with eye contact]**

> A selective LDL-receptor lesion, first described by Brown and Goldstein in their Nobel Prize work in 1985, confirmed in 13,447 propensity-matched participants forty years later. The biology is the same. The data is new. That green panel — Panel F — is the engine of TUDOR.

**[Time check: approximately 12:45-13:00]**

## T — THE MODEL: What TUDOR Is (13:00-16:30)

**[Show the "Three ideas, Eleven variables" slide]**

> So we wrapped that biology into a model. And I want to be clear about what TUDOR is and what it is not.

> TUDOR is a 10-feature elastic-net logistic regression. Alpha 0.5, fully locked coefficients. It is not a black box. It is not deep learning. It is not a neural network. It is an interpretable equation you could write on a napkin and deploy in any lab information system on the planet. That is a feature, not a limitation.

> The model is built on three ideas:

**[Hold up one finger]**

> Idea one: Lipid Age. "What would this patient's LDL be without treatment?" We reconstruct pre-treatment LDL using real-world, drug-specific, dose-adjusted statin correction factors — not trial efficacy numbers, but what actually happens in the population. Atorvastatin 25 to 48 percent reduction. Rosuvastatin 35 to 55 percent. Simvastatin 20 to 42 percent. Ezetimibe adds 20 percent. PCSK9 inhibitors up to 65 percent, capped at 85 percent. These reflect actual adherence, not the compliance of a monitored trial participant.

**[Two fingers]**

> Idea two: Triglyceride Shield. The molecular asymmetry we just proved. The Trig Filter — LDL divided by triglycerides plus 0.1 — separates monogenic isolated-LDL FH from mixed metabolic dyslipidaemia. This is the novel biological signal. No existing FH diagnostic tool uses it.

**[Three fingers]**

> Idea three: Proband Effect. Were they found by clinical ascertainment — the proband who presented with an MI — or quietly picked up through cascade screening? Because the cascade-screened relative carries the same pathogenic variant but presents with an attenuated phenotype. The model needs to know how the patient entered the system.

**[Gesture at the coefficient bar chart]**

> Eleven coefficients, locked and frozen from the development cohort. The top bar — treated LDL — pulls hardest. The red bars are the triglyceride shield dragging the score down when the picture turns mixed. Two ideas explain ten coefficients. The third explains how the cohorts were split. That is the whole machine.

> Comparators — DLCN, Simon Broome, MEDPED — were pre-registered and applied as published rules with no refitting. The sign convention was locked before any metric was computed: Delta AUC equals TUDOR minus comparator. Positive favours TUDOR. This was written down before we saw a single number.

**[Time check: approximately 16:00-16:30]**

## H — HOW WE TESTED IT: Validation Design (16:30-19:30)

**[Show the validation flow slide: South Wales -> Whole Wales -> UK Biobank]**

> Then we tried to break it. Three times, each one further from home.

> Stage one: South Wales. 3,099 patients. 554 confirmed LDLR carriers. Cardiff and Vale catchment. This is where the model was born. TRIPOD type 1b — development cohort.

> Stage two: Whole of Wales. The entire All-Wales FH Registry, PASS system. Geographic split — not random. 4,570 patients. 887 LDLR carriers. Same equation, frozen coefficients, nothing re-estimated. TRIPOD type 3 — geographic external validation. It held.

**[Slow down for the big number]**

> Stage three: UK Biobank. 501,936 participants. 3,540 LDLR carriers identified by whole-exome sequencing. Different country. Different decade. Different ascertainment — population genotyping, not clinical referral. Same locked equation, dropped in cold. TRIPOD type 4 — the strictest possible external validation. The model had never seen a single Biobank participant.

> Combined: 506,506 adults. To our knowledge, one of the largest genetically confirmed FH diagnostic validation studies reported.

> The design is bidirectional. Wales validates in Biobank. Biobank validates back in Wales. If the signal only works in one direction, it might be an artefact of ascertainment. If it survives both directions, it is more likely real biology.

**[Grin — deliver with warmth]**

> For context — the last time a Tudor crossed the Severn to take on the rest of Britain, in 1485, he did it with considerably less peer review.

**[Beat for laugh, then pivot back to serious]**

> Family-level deduplication. Zero shared identifiers between development and validation. MICE imputation. Z-score standardisation. Every reported number traces to a locked, reproducible analysis file.

> We do not claim TUDOR replaces genetic testing. We claim it refines pre-test probability for genetic testing. It tells you who to test first. And in a world where genetic testing capacity is limited, prioritisation is everything.

### Limitations (26:30-28:30)

**[Mnemonic: OWN IT]**

> Let me close with limitations, because I would rather say them than have you find them.

> O — Observational. This is retrospective external validation, not a prospective clinical-impact trial. Strong signal, but it does not yet prove that using TUDOR changes outcomes.

> W — Weights are fixed. The model was deliberately not recalibrated on UK Biobank. That is methodologically correct for TRIPOD Type 4 — but it means the absolute probabilities may need local recalibration before deployment in other health systems.

> N — Not all populations tested. UK Biobank has well-documented healthy-volunteer bias. The model needs validation in more diverse, less selected populations. We acknowledge that explicitly.

> I — Incomplete eDLCN. I have been honest throughout: the comparison to DLCN is generous to TUDOR. In population data, DLCN cannot use family history, xanthomata, or arcus — it loses roughly half its scoring capacity. TUDOR was designed for that setting, but the comparison is structurally unfair and I want every person in this room to hear me say so.

> T — Treatment history is approximate. Back-calculated untreated LDL-C is useful but not perfect. Dose, duration, adherence, and absorption variability all introduce noise. The model's robustness to plus-or-minus 10 percent variation in statin correction factors was formally tested and held — but it is an approximation, not a time machine.

# PART 2: MODERATOR Q&A — 10 QUESTIONS WITH EXACT ANSWERS

## Q1: "Your UKB AUC is only 0.538. How is that clinically meaningful?"

> Excellent question, and I am glad you asked because I consider this the most important number to contextualise. In a population of half a million with a 0.7 percent FH prevalence, all three comparators — DLCN, MEDPED, Simon Broome — score at or below 0.50, which is random chance. TUDOR's value is not the absolute AUC in isolation. It is the consistent positive delta across all 25 prespecified subgroups — no exceptions — and the NRI showing 190 additional carriers found. At population scale, a tool that reliably beats chance identifies thousands of patients that chance-level tools structurally cannot.

## Q2: "You are comparing against a truncated eDLCN. That is not fair."

> You are absolutely right, and I said so in the talk. eDLCN in population data cannot use family history, xanthomata, or corneal arcus — it loses roughly half its scoring capacity. But that is precisely the point: in the setting where population-scale screening would actually be deployed — electronic health records, biobanks, primary care — those features are unavailable. The comparison is unfair to eDLCN, but it is realistic to the use case. TUDOR was designed for the world where the physical examination has already disappeared.

## Q3: "This is logistic regression. Why call it machine learning?"

> Strictly, elastic-net regularisation is a machine-learning technique — it performs automated feature selection and coefficient shrinkage via cross-validated penalisation. But the final deployed model is an 11-coefficient logistic equation, and I would not call it deep learning or a black box. I consider the simplicity a strength: it is interpretable, transportable, and deployable inside any laboratory information system. We chose the simplest model that outperformed, not the most complex model we could fit.

## Q4: "How do you handle statin dose uncertainty?"

> UKB medication data captures drug name but dose is variable across intensity bands. We use published intensity ranges — atorvastatin 25 to 48 percent, rosuvastatin 35 to 55 percent — and the model is tested with a formal sensitivity analysis at plus and minus 10 percent variation in these factors. The AUC is robust across that range. Dose-level correction from electronic prescribing records would likely improve precision further; it is a data limitation, not a methodological one, and we address it explicitly in the sensitivity analyses.

## Q5: "What about polygenic hypercholesterolaemia? Your non-FH controls include them."

> Correct, and that actually makes our result more conservative. The non-FH control group contains polygenic hypercholesterolaemia, metabolic-syndrome hyperlipidaemia, secondary causes, and lifestyle-driven elevation. TUDOR is discriminating monogenic FH from this entire mixture — which is the harder task. If we removed the polygenics, performance would likely improve. We deliberately left them in because that is the real-world clinical question: "this patient has high LDL — is it monogenic or something else?"

## Q6: "Why not use random forest or XGBoost — would that not give better AUC?"

> Potentially, in sample. But we prioritised three things: interpretability, so clinicians can understand each coefficient; transportability, so the equation deploys in any system without requiring a software platform; and resistance to overfitting in small development cohorts. A 10-feature logistic model with elastic-net regularisation gives us all three. We have comparison analyses against random forest and gradient boosting in the supplementary pipeline, and the logistic model performs comparably with far lower implementation complexity.

## Q7: "How do you define your gold standard — is ClinVar reliable enough?"

> We used ClinVar pathogenic and likely pathogenic variants in LDLR, APOB, and PCSK9. ClinVar classifications do evolve, and we state the version used. For LDLR coding variants in UK Biobank, the ascertainment is whole-exome sequencing, which provides good coverage of coding regions. One limitation we acknowledge is that large structural variants — copy number variants — in LDLR, which account for roughly 10 percent of FH mutations, may be missed by array-based or short-read sequencing. This could lead to some true carriers being misclassified as controls, which would make our performance estimates conservative.

## Q8: "What about ezetimibe and PCSK9 inhibitor correction?"

> Both are included. Ezetimibe adds approximately 20 percent on top of statin correction. PCSK9 inhibitors add up to 65 percent, capped at 85 percent total for combination therapy. These are applied dose-specifically and drug-specifically. I should note that PCSK9 inhibitor use in UKB is relatively low given the recruitment era, so this correction applies to a smaller subgroup — but it is there.

## Q9: "Would this work in a US population? Different ethnicities, different prescribing."

> The clinical logic should transport well — the underlying biology of the Trig Filter is receptor-mechanism-based, not population-specific. Brown-Goldstein selectivity does not vary by ethnicity. However, absolute calibration should not be assumed. Prescribing patterns, adherence rates, and population prevalence differ. A US validation cohort would be essential before any deployment. We have formally tested ethnicity stratification in our sensitivity analyses where UKB data permits, and the signal direction is preserved, though some subgroups have limited case numbers.

## Q10: "What is the next step?"

> Three things. First, prospective validation — a clinical implementation study where TUDOR is deployed in real lipid clinics and we measure whether it changes referral for genetic testing and ultimately diagnosis rates. Second, diverse population validation — non-UKB, non-European cohorts. Third, integration with the ApoB-based dyslipidaemia classification framework — using ApoB as a gating variable to further refine the Trig Filter signal in borderline cases. The model is ready for testing. The next question is whether it changes practice.
