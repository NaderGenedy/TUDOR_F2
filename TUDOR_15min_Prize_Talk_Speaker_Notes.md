## SLIDE 1 — Henry VIII / Title (~40s)

"So I named a familial hypercholesterolaemia algorithm after a Tudor king. [gesture at Henry] And before anyone in cardiology objects — yes, I'm aware Henry VIII had the lipid profile of a man who'd never met a vegetable. [beat for laugh] But here's the thing about the Tudors: they took a messy, fragmented kingdom and unified it under one rule. That's the job. One equation, every patient, across half a million people.

I'm Nader Genedy, metabolic medicine, Cardiff. And this is TUDOR — the algorithm we built to find the most common deadly genetic disease in medicine, the one we still miss nine times out of ten."

## SLIDE 3 — Why FH Matters (~70s)

"Quick honesty exercise — hands up, no shame: who's diagnosed a new FH case in the last month? [count] Now keep it up if you used a formal DLCN score to do it. [hands drop] Right. Hold that thought, because that's the entire talk.

FH is about 1 in 250. More common than type 1 diabetes. In a room this size, the maths says a couple of us are carrying it right now and don't know. [let it land]

This is Beheshti's meta-analysis. Watch the curve — prevalence climbs through midlife, peaks around 0.35 percent... and then falls. [beat] Now, FH is genetic. It doesn't resolve. It doesn't get bored and leave. So why does prevalence drop after 70? [pause — let them answer in their heads] Because the carriers aren't there anymore. That falling tail isn't recovery. It's a body count. The inverted U IS the mortality signal.

And the part that should genuinely annoy you: Luirink, New England Journal — start a statin in childhood, you restore near-normal cardiovascular outcomes. We have the cure. It's generic. It's pennies. We just can't find the patients."

---

## SLIDE 4 — We Had Tools. The World Changed Under Them. (~75s)

"So why do we miss them? This slide is my favourite, so indulge me. [point top row] Top timeline — our diagnostic criteria. Simon Broome, '91. MEDPED, '93. Dutch Lipid Clinic, WHO, '99. [point bottom row] Bottom timeline — the drugs. And look what happens after 1994. Statins. Then ezetimibe. Then PCSK9 inhibitors. Then inclisiran. Then bempedoic acid.

Here's the irony that keeps me up at night. The criteria lean on tendon xanthomata and dramatic untreated LDLs. And then we got spectacularly good at lowering LDL. We cured the clue. [beat] We invented better and better erasers for the exact phenotype our diagnostic tools were trained to spot.

[slow down, drop the voice] So the punchline writes itself: 'The criteria are excellent. The patients they were calibrated for are no longer the patients we see.' [pause] That's not a dig at the criteria. They were right — for 1995. The patient walked off and the tool stayed put."

## SLIDE 6 — The Contemporary Patient (~80s)

"So who IS the patient now? Meet them. Left, Wales FH Registry probands. Right, UK Biobank lipid-clinic-eligible. Four panels, four gut-punches.

[point] Diabetes — climbing in Wales to twenty-seven percent. BMI — everybody's hovering around 29; we're all a bit rounder than we'd like, the patients included. Triglycerides — both cohorts converging north of 2. And the finale — [point] sixty-two to eighty-one percent are already on a statin before we've laid a finger on the diagnosis.

Picture the textbook FH patient: skinny, untreated, LDL of 9, a xanthoma you could hang a coat on. [beat] Now picture who actually shows up: overweight, often diabetic, triglycerides up, already three years into atorvastatin. [beat] That patient is kryptonite to every classical criterion. And that's not a rare edge case. That's the median Tuesday."

## SLIDE 7 — Three Tools. Four Subgroups. One Finding. (~85s)

"So we did the obvious experiment. Took the big three — DLCN, MEDPED, Simon Broome — and ran them head-to-head in the populations the modern lipid clinic actually sees. This dashed line [point] is 0.5. Chance. A coin.

[sweep left to right] Young patients — all three flirting with the coin. On a statin at first visit — DLCN limps over the line, MEDPED face-plants. Mixed dyslipidaemia — the high-LDL, high-trig crowd — all three down. And then type 2 diabetes — forty-five thousand Biobank participants — DLCN scores 0.435. [beat] Let that sit. That's not 'underwhelming.' That's worse than a coin. You'd improve your diagnostic accuracy by firing the tool and flipping a 10p piece.

[calm, respectful — this is the judo] I'll say it plainly: in the patients we actually see, all three established tools are no better than chance. I'm not here to embarrass three excellent instruments. I'm here to point out the room they were built for has been remodelled."

## SLIDE 8 — The Molecular Asymmetry (~85s)

"Now the fun part. If the old signals are gone, what's left? This. [point]

We took every untreated Biobank participant, sorted them into LDL bands, and inside each band compared carriers — red — to non-carriers — navy. Same LDL. The question is: is there anything that separates them?

[trace the hero panel] Triglycerides. Every band, from under 1.4 right up past 4.9 — the red bar sits below the navy. Carriers run lower triglycerides at every single LDL level. Same signal, top to bottom.

[point down] And the controls: VLDL — unchanged. HDL — unchanged. [beat] So at identical LDL, the one thing that betrays an FH carrier is that their triglycerides are suspiciously, consistently, beautifully low. That's Brown and Goldstein — the receptor lesion is selective. It snaps LDL clearance and leaves the triglyceride machinery humming. The Trig Filter — LDL over triglycerides — just reads that fingerprint off a routine panel. No genetics. No xanthoma. A blood test you already ordered."

---

## SLIDE 9 — Brown-Goldstein, Confounder-Matched (~70s)

"Now, a good sceptic is already muttering 'confounding.' Fair. So we matched for it. Propensity-matched carriers to non-carriers, one to five, on age, sex and BMI — thirteen and a half thousand people, balanced to a fault.

[rapid, confident] After matching: LDL — up in carriers, p of ten-to-the-minus-twenty-eight. Triglycerides — unchanged. HDL — bang on unchanged, p of 0.99, you couldn't ask for cleaner. And the Trig Filter — [land on Panel F, slow] — up ten percent in carriers. p of five-times-ten-to-the-minus-twenty-five.

[beat] A selective LDL-receptor lesion, predicted from Nobel Prize work in 1985, sitting there confirmed in thirteen thousand matched people in 2026. Same biology. New data. That green panel is the entire engine of TUDOR."

---

## SLIDE 10 — Three Ideas. Eleven Variables. One Equation. (~80s)

"So we wrapped that biology into a model. And I promise you it's not a black box — it's three ideas a registrar could explain at handover.

[idea one] Lipid Age. 'What would this LDL be if we hadn't treated them?' We rewind the statin — real-world, drug-specific corrections — to recover the untreated number.

[idea two] Triglyceride Shield. The asymmetry we just proved. Clean isolated LDL, or messy mixed picture?

[idea three] Proband Effect. Were they found by clinical drama, or quietly picked up by cascade screening? Because the cousin found by screening carries the same gene but a politer phenotype — and the model needs to know that.

[gesture at bars] Eleven coefficients, locked and frozen. Treated LDL pulls hardest [point top bar]; the red bars are the triglyceride shield dragging the score down when the picture turns mixed. [beat] Two ideas explain ten of the coefficients. The third explains how we split the cohorts. That's the whole machine."

[Don't read the hyperparameters aloud — save alpha/saga for Q&A.]

## SLIDE 11 — Developed in Wales, Validated in 506,506 (~75s)

"Then we tried to break it. Three times, each one further from home.

[stage one] South Wales. Three thousand patients, 554 carriers. Where it was born.

[stage two] The whole of Wales — and crucially, a geographic split, not a random one. New region, frozen equation, nothing re-fit. It held.

[stage three] UK Biobank. Half a million people. Three and a half thousand carriers by whole-exome sequencing. Different country, different decade, different way of finding patients. Same locked equation, dropped in cold. TRIPOD type 4 — the strictest external test there is.

[beat, grin] For context — the last time a Tudor crossed the Severn to take on the rest of Britain, in 1485, he did it with considerably less peer review. We brought a pre-registered protocol."

## SLIDE 12 — 25 of 25 Prespecified Subgroups (~90s)

"Results. Pre-registered, head-to-head, against all three tools, across twenty-five subgroups we named in advance.

[top panel] Wales primary: TUDOR 0.770, DLCN 0.670. Full Biobank: TUDOR 0.538. [stop — own it, eye contact] And I'm going to say the quiet part out loud, because you're all thinking it: 0.538 is modest. In a clean registry I'd be embarrassed. But look who it's standing next to — DLCN 0.510, MEDPED 0.505, Simon Broome 0.505. Three coins. In a population of half a million at 0.7 percent prevalence, the meaningful question isn't 'is it 0.9' — it's 'does anything beat the coin.' One thing does.

[bottom panel — the win] And this is what I'd frame on my wall. Twenty-five subgroups. Every. Single. Delta. Positive. Eleven of eleven in Wales, fourteen of fourteen in Biobank. Seventy-five out of seventy-five head-to-head comparisons — [beat] — and not one loss. I went looking for a subgroup where TUDOR loses, honestly hoping to find one to keep me humble. Couldn't. [small shrug]

[three columns] For the lipid clinic — catches what DLCN drops. For the cardiologist — flags your treated diabetics. For primary care — nobody has to go hunting for an Achilles xanthoma ever again. [beat] You're welcome."

## SLIDE 13 — Clinical Impact: The Patients DLCN Misses (~80s)

"But AUC doesn't save anyone. Patients do. So — per hundred carriers in each subgroup, how many extra does TUDOR catch that DLCN walks straight past?

[sweep] Type 2 diabetes — twenty-five more per hundred. Cascade lone-index cases — fourteen. Mixed dyslipidaemia — fourteen. The young — thirteen. Already on a statin — ten.

Add it up across both cohorts: a hundred and ninety extra carriers found. [beat] And FH is the gift that keeps giving — every index case opens a family. Cascade four relatives, number-needed-to-test of about two, and those 190 become roughly two hundred and eighty-five people who now know, who get a statin, who don't have the heart attack at 45.

[read it slow] Where DLCN fails — the young, the treated, the metabolically messy, the diabetic, the quiet cascade case — that is exactly where TUDOR closes the gap. It's strongest precisely where the old tools are weakest."

## DIRECTOR'S NOTES

The one fix you cannot skip: that "0.538, own it" beat on Slide 12. The entire credibility of the talk hinges on you saying the modest number out loud, with a steady face, before a judge can pounce. Practise it until it sounds like confidence, not apology. The moment you flinch, you lose the room; the moment you own it, you own them.

The funny is load-bearing, not decoration. Every joke here is also a fact: the vegetable line sets up phenotype, the 10p coin IS the AUC argument, the Severn gag IS the validation escalation, the xanthoma "you're welcome" IS the primary-care use case. If a laugh ever stops teaching, cut it — in a prize room, a joke that's just a joke reads as nerves.

Fix "dictated" to "dedicated" on your final slide before you present.
